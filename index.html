Prep Guide

How to Use This Guide

  • Buy proteins first and portion them before refrigerating or freezing.
  • Quantities on the shopping list are rounded to practical store sizes.
  • The dinner-to-next-lunch system means you only cook one extra portion each evening — no separate lunch cooking needed.

Sunday Prep Session — approx. 90 minutes

1. Portion proteins

Meat, fish, and mince portions are listed as raw weight before cooking. Tuna is listed as drained weight, and dairy items are listed as served weight.

  • Chicken breast → 2 × 170g raw portions (Day 1 dinner & Day 2 lunch) + 2 × 180g raw portions (Day 4 dinner & Day 5 lunch)
  • Beef mince → 2 × 170g raw portions (Day 2 dinner & Day 3 lunch)
  • Hake → 2 × 180g raw portions (Day 3 dinner & Day 4 lunch)
  • Salmon fillets → 2 × 160g raw portions (Day 5 dinner & Day 6 lunch)
  • Sirloin steak → 2 × 170g raw portions (Day 6 dinner & Day 7 lunch)
  • Chicken breast → extra 180g raw strips for Day 7 dinner

2. Wash and chop vegetables

  • Chop broccoli, carrots, butternut, courgettes, peppers, onions, and green beans
  • Wash salad leaves; store with paper towel in a sealed container
  • Slice cucumber; prep beetroot if not using a pre-cooked pack
  • Keep mushrooms and tomatoes separate for quick breakfasts

3. Cook base carbs

  • Brown rice → 2 × 100g cooked portions
  • Basmati rice → 2 × 100g cooked portions
  • Quinoa → 2 × 100g cooked portions for Days 4 & 5 lunch; cook 2 more on Day 5 evening for dinner & Day 6 lunch
  • Pre-boil or roast sweet potatoes and baby potatoes for the first half of the week
  • Measure dry oats into containers for overnight oats (Day 3) and shake add-in (Day 6)

4. Breakfast and snack pre-packing

  • Boil 2 eggs for Day 2 snack
  • Build overnight oats for Day 3 breakfast — assemble in a jar the night before
  • Portion Greek yogurt, cottage cheese, almonds, Provitas, and rice cakes into grab-and-go servings
  • Keep fruit washed and visible for easier adherence

Midweek Top-Up — approx. 45–60 minutes (Day 4 or evening before)

  • Roast potato wedges for Day 6 if not done earlier
  • Prep gem squash and extra salad vegetables
  • Marinate sirloin steak and chicken breast strips for Day 7
  • Refill snack portions for the second half of the week

Portion raw proteins before refrigerating. Freeze what you will not use in the first 2–3 days and thaw in the fridge overnight.

  • Lean pork mince → 2 × 170g raw portions (Day 2 dinner & Day 3 lunch)

  • Basmati rice → 2 × 100g cooked portions for Days 2 & 3

  • Measure oats into a jar for overnight oats (Day 3 — build the evening before)

  • Boil 2 eggs for Day 2 snack and 2 more for Day 7 snack
  • Build overnight oats for Day 3 the evening before (Day 2 night)
  • Portion Greek yogurt, cottage cheese, almonds, Provitas, and rice cakes into grab-and-go servings
  • Wash all fruit and keep it visible at the front of the fridge

  • Prep potato wedges for Days 5 & 6 (par-boil and season ready to roast)
  • Marinate steak and pork fillet strips if preferred

Light Exercise Guide

You do not need to train hard to lose fat. Consistent light-to-moderate movement combined with this meal plan is a highly effective and sustainable combination. The exercises below are low-impact, easy to start, and scale as your fitness improves.

Why Light Exercise Works for Fat Loss

  • Low-intensity exercise burns fat directly as its primary fuel source — your body preferentially uses stored fat at moderate effort levels.
  • It preserves muscle better than aggressive cardio, which matters because muscle drives your resting metabolism.
  • It is sustainable — you can do it daily without needing recovery days or risking injury.
  • It compounds: 45 minutes of walking every day is more valuable over a month than 3 brutal sessions followed by exhaustion and a week off.
  • Daily movement outside structured sessions (NEAT — Non-Exercise Activity Thermogenesis) such as taking the stairs, parking further away, and standing more can add up to several hundred extra calories burned per day without a single gym session.

Understanding Your Training Zones

A rough estimate of your maximum heart rate (MHR) is 220 minus your age. Use this to gauge effort during exercise.

Zone% of Max HRHow it feelsBest for
Zone 1 — Easy50–60%Very light; can hold a full conversation easilyRecovery walks, warm-up, daily steps
Zone 2 — Fat Burn60–70%Comfortable; can talk in full sentences but slightly aware of breathingMain fat-loss zone; walking, easy cycling
Zone 3 — Aerobic70–80%Moderate effort; can speak in short sentences onlyImproved fitness; brisk walking, moderate cycling
Zone 4 — Threshold80–90%Hard; speaking is difficultInterval work; short bursts only

For fat loss at the beginner-to-intermediate level, Zone 2 is your target for most sessions. It feels almost too easy — that is correct.

Walking

Walking is underrated. It is low impact, requires no equipment, and is one of the most studied and effective fat-loss tools available.

Daily steps target

  • Aim for 8 000–10 000 steps per day as a baseline. This does not need to be a single walk — it accumulates through your day.
  • If you are currently below 5 000 steps, do not jump straight to 10 000. Add 1 000–1 500 steps per week until you build the habit.

Tips

  • Fasted morning walks (before breakfast, with only water or black coffee) are particularly effective for fat oxidation — your glycogen stores are low and your body draws on fat earlier.
  • Walk with a purpose: pick a destination, use it as phone-free time, or listen to a podcast. Something that makes you want to go.
  • Incline walking at a slow speed burns significantly more calories than flat walking and is easier on the joints than running.
  • Track your steps with your phone or a watch — awareness alone tends to increase daily movement.

Trainer / Stationary Cycling

An indoor trainer or stationary bike is excellent for this plan — joint-friendly, no weather dependency, and easy to control intensity precisely.

Cadence and resistance guidance

  • A cadence of 80–95 rpm at moderate resistance is the sweet spot for fat burning and joint health. Higher cadence at lower resistance is easier on your knees than grinding a heavy gear slowly.
  • If you have a power meter or heart rate monitor, target Zone 2 (<70% MHR) for the majority of each session.
  • If you are going by feel: you should be able to breathe through your nose for most of a Zone 2 session.

Tips

  • Put the bike somewhere visible and convenient — friction is the enemy of consistency.
  • Ride while watching a show or listening to music. The time passes faster and you are more likely to do it.
  • Clip-in or cage pedals are not necessary for light sessions — any comfortable shoes work on a stationary trainer.
  • Do not skip the warm-up. Even 5 minutes of easy spinning before increasing effort makes the session feel much better.

  • Short sessions under 45 minutes: no extra food needed. Stick to the meal plan as written.
  • Fasted morning walks: water or black coffee beforehand only. Eat your normal breakfast afterwards.
  • Sessions 45–75 minutes: if the session falls more than 3 hours after your last meal, a small snack beforehand can help — 2 Provitas with cottage cheese or a rice cake with peanut butter is enough.
  • Sessions over 75 minutes: have a full meal 1.5–2 hours before and include a moderate carb portion (rice, oats, or potato) in that meal.
  • After exercise: protein within 1–2 hours supports recovery. The meal plan already provides this — no separate protein shake is required unless the session falls in a gap between meals.
  • Do not reward exercise with extra food unless the session was genuinely long and intense (>75 min). Light exercise burns less than most people expect, and eating back those calories is the most common reason light activity fails to produce fat loss.

Progression Over Time

  • Weeks 1–2: establish the habit. 3–4 sessions per week of 20–40 minutes each. Prioritise showing up over intensity.
  • Weeks 3–4: extend sessions by 5–10 minutes. Add one additional session per week if you feel recovered.
  • Month 2: introduce one slightly harder session per week (incline walk, interval cycling, or a longer Zone 2 ride).
  • Month 3+: consider adding light resistance training 2× per week alongside cardio. Retaining and building a small amount of muscle is one of the most effective long-term fat-loss strategies because muscle raises your resting metabolic rate.
  • If you feel consistently fatigued, reduce volume before reducing food. Fatigue usually means too much exercise or not enough sleep, not too much food.

Kelfit Overview Summary

Goal

Both plans are designed for sustainable fat loss: enough protein to support muscle retention, enough carbohydrates to keep training and daily energy practical, and enough vegetable volume to improve fullness without making the plan feel restrictive.

Nutrition Structure

  • Protein appears at every main meal and most snacks.
  • Meat, fish, and mince portions are listed as raw weight before cooking; tuna is drained weight, and dairy is served weight.
  • Carbs are moderate and portioned: cooked rice or quinoa is generally 100g, potatoes are 140–150g, and oats are 30–45g.
  • Vegetable portions are intentionally large, usually 250–300g with lunch and dinner.
  • Higher-calorie extras such as peanut butter, avocado, nuts, seeds, feta, hummus, salmon, steak, and olive oil should stay measured.

Meal Prep System

  • Dinner is always cooked as two portions — the second portion becomes lunch the next day.
  • USN Diet Fuel Ultralean is included on selected days for convenience, not as the foundation of the whole plan.
  • Provitas, rice cakes, Greek yogurt, cottage cheese, fruit, eggs, and biltong provide repeatable snack options across both weeks.
  • Airfryer and stovetop meals are both included so the plan stays usable across different cooking setups.

Can You Swap Days Around?

  • Yes — the days have no fixed order. The plan works equally well whether you follow Monday–Sunday as written or rearrange days to suit your week.
  • The one rule to keep in mind: each dinner and the following day’s lunch are a matched pair — dinner is cooked as a double portion so the next day’s lunch is already done. If you swap days, either move the dinner and its paired lunch together, or plan to cook lunch fresh on that day instead of using leftovers.
  • Days can also be mixed freely between Week 1 and Week 2 — all days across both plans are built on the same caloric and macro structure, so combining them does not break the plan.
  • If a specific protein is unavailable or not preferred on a given day, use any day from the same week or an equivalent from the Alternatives tab — the overall balance for the week stays intact.

Rotating Between Week 1 and Week 2

  • Week 2 rotates the protein sources — trout, lean pork mince, and lean pork fillet strips replace some of Week 1’s proteins to reduce repetition while keeping the same structure.
  • Run Week 1 and Week 2 on alternate weeks for variety without needing to plan new meals each time.
  • Both weeks share the same shopping staples (oats, Greek yogurt, cottage cheese, eggs, rice, quinoa, vegetables), so pantry stock carries over.
  • Individual days can be swapped freely between weeks — both plans use the same caloric structure.

Fat-Loss Adjustment Rule

  • If hunger is high, increase non-starchy vegetables first.
  • If energy is low around training, move a fruit or carb portion closer to the workout instead of adding extra snacks.
  • If weight is not trending down after 2–3 consistent weeks, reduce one calorie-dense extra or slightly reduce a carb portion before cutting protein.
  • If weight loss is too fast or adherence feels poor, add a small carb or yogurt-based snack and keep the meal structure consistent.

Drinks Guide

What you drink matters almost as much as what you eat. This guide covers every common drink and how it fits — or doesn’t — into the Kelfit plan.

Water — the foundation

  • Aim for 2–3 litres per day minimum; more on training days or in hot weather.
  • Start each morning with a large glass (300–500ml) before anything else — it reduces false hunger signals.
  • Keep a bottle visible during the day; most people underdrink simply because it is out of sight.
  • Plain sparkling or still water are both fine — choose whichever you will actually drink more of.
  • Add a slice of lemon, cucumber, or a few mint leaves if plain water feels unappealing — zero calories and it works.

Coffee

  • Black coffee — best option. Zero calories, mild appetite suppression, and small fat oxidation benefit. Drink freely up to 2–3 cups per day.
  • With a small splash of low-fat milk (30–50ml) — adds roughly 15–25 kcal and about 1.5g protein. Perfectly fine as a daily habit.
  • With full-cream milk or creamer — adds up quickly across multiple cups. Either switch to low-fat milk or count it as part of your dairy intake for the day.
  • Cappuccinos and flat whites at a café — a standard milk-based coffee is 80–130 kcal. One per day is manageable; two or more per day can quietly stall progress.
  • Avoid: sugar, flavoured syrups, whipped cream, caramel or vanilla lattes. These turn a zero-calorie drink into a dessert.
  • If you train in the morning, a black coffee 20–30 minutes beforehand works well as a low-cost pre-workout.

Rooibos Tea — ideal everyday drink

  • Zero calories, zero caffeine, high in antioxidants, and it is a genuine SA staple — drink as much as you like.
  • A small splash of low-fat milk is fine; just skip the sugar.
  • Works particularly well in the evening when you want something warm without the caffeine keeping you awake.
  • Red espresso (rooibos concentrate) is a good coffee alternative if you are trying to cut back on caffeine.

English (Black) Tea

  • Black tea brewed plain is near zero calories and a good antioxidant source.
  • A small splash of low-fat milk (30ml) is the traditional way and keeps calories negligible.
  • Contains caffeine — roughly 40–70mg per cup (less than coffee). Stick to 2–3 cups per day, especially if you are sensitive to caffeine or drink it in the afternoon.
  • Avoid sweetening with sugar; use cinnamon or a small amount of unsweetened vanilla if you need flavour.

Green Tea

  • Excellent choice — zero calories plain, good antioxidant profile (EGCG), and light caffeine (25–40mg per cup).
  • There is modest research support for a small boost to fat oxidation at rest, particularly when consumed consistently.
  • Best drunk as is; adding milk reduces the antioxidant absorption. A slice of lemon is a good addition instead.
  • 2–3 cups per day is a sensible amount.

Chai Tea

  • Spiced black tea brewed plain (cinnamon, cardamom, ginger, clove) — zero calories, great flavour. Drink freely.
  • Chai brewed with low-fat milk and no sugar — adds protein and calcium, still a reasonable choice. Count the milk portion.
  • Chai latte (café-style or from a pod/sachet) — often contains 10–20g of added sugar and full-cream milk. This is effectively a dessert drink. Either make it yourself with low-fat milk and no sweetener, or treat it as an occasional extra rather than a daily habit.
  • Pre-made chai concentrate products (e.g. Oregon Chai) are usually very high in sugar — check the label before using.

Other Herbal & Fruit Teas

  • Chamomile, peppermint, ginger, hibiscus, honeybush, nettle — all zero calories and unlimited.
  • Ginger tea is particularly useful for digestion and appetite management.
  • Avoid commercially sweetened fruit teas or iced teas (e.g. Lipton Ice Tea, Nestea) — these are sugar water dressed up as tea and can contain 20–35g sugar per bottle.
  • Homemade iced rooibos or green tea chilled in the fridge with lemon and mint is a zero-calorie alternative to commercial iced tea.

Alcohol

  • Alcohol directly pauses fat metabolism — your body prioritises clearing alcohol before processing anything else, so fat burning stops while it is in your system.
  • A standard 340ml beer is roughly 150 kcal; a 175ml glass of wine is 120–150 kcal; a single spirit (35ml) with full-sugar mixer is 120–180 kcal.
  • Alcohol also reduces sleep quality and recovery, increases the likelihood of poor food choices the next day, and raises cortisol, which can increase fat storage around the midsection.
  • If you do drink, the lower-impact options are: dry red or white wine (1 glass), spirits (brandy, whisky, vodka, rum) with sparkling water or diet soda (no sugary mixer), or a light beer.
  • Avoid: sweet ciders, cocktails with juice or syrup, shooters, and more than 1–2 drinks at a time.
  • Practical rule: treat a drinking occasion as a choice to pause progress for that day, limit food fat and carb intake on the same day, increase water intake before and after, and return to plan the next morning without guilt.

Diet / Zero-Sugar Drinks

  • Zero-calorie technically — Coke Zero, Pepsi Max, sugar-free Fanta, etc. They will not break a calorie deficit on their own.
  • However, artificial sweeteners (aspartame, sucralose, acesulfame-K) maintain a sweet preference and can trigger cravings for real sugar later in the day in some people.
  • Use them as a transition tool to cut out regular sodas, but work towards preferring sparkling water or plain drinks over time.
  • If you find they are increasing your appetite or sweet cravings, drop them. If they make no difference, occasional use is fine.
